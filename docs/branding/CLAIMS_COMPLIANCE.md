# CONKA Claims Compliance Reference

> **Purpose:** Legal reference for auditing all customer-facing copy. Every claim on the website must pass the three-part test at the bottom of this document.
> **Scope:** UK law (England). The substantive rules are the same across all four nations as they derive from retained EU law.
> **Last updated:** 2026-03-31

---

## Governing regulations

| Regulation | What it governs |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Food Safety Act 1990** | General prohibition on false/misleading presentation of food. Strict liability. Applies to website copy, imagery, and overall impression. |
| **Retained Regulation (EC) No. 1924/2006** | The core claims regulation. Defines what health/nutrition claims are, which are permitted (EFSA register only), and which are prohibited. |
| **Nutrition and Health Claims (England) Regulations 2007** | Domestic enforcement of 1924/2006. Creates criminal offences and penalties. |
| **The Food Supplements (England) Regulations 2003** | Labelling requirements for supplements sold online (distance selling). Mandatory statements on product pages. |
| **Regulation (EU) No. 1169/2011 (retained UK law)** | Food information to consumers. Prohibits misleading presentation, cherry-picking of characteristics, false special properties. |
| **CAP Code (ASA)** | Advertising standards for non-broadcast (websites, social, paid ads, email). Enforces 1924/2006 in advertising context. |

---

## Enforcement bodies and consequences

| Body | Powers |
|------|--------|
| **Trading Standards** | Criminal prosecution, unlimited fines, imprisonment up to 2 years, product seizure |
| **Food Standards Agency (FSA)** | Policy oversight, directs Trading Standards, novel food authorisations |
| **ASA / CAP** | Published rulings, platform ad bans, referral to Trading Standards |
| **MHRA** | If claims cross into medicinal territory: forced product withdrawal, criminal prosecution, product destruction |

**The MHRA risk:** If copy claims the product diagnoses, treats, cures, or prevents disease, the MHRA can classify it as an unauthorised medicine. This is the most severe outcome.

## What is a "claim"?

A claim is any message or representation -- including text, imagery, graphics, symbols, layout, or overall impression -- that states, suggests, or implies the food has particular characteristics or a relationship with health.

**The "suggests or implies" test is critical.** You don't need to use prohibited words. If the overall impression implies a health benefit, it's caught:
- "Designed for people struggling with brain fog" -- implies treatment of a condition
- "When your mind won't focus" followed by product imagery -- implies the product fixes a symptom
- Before/after framing suggesting resolution of a health issue
- Brain imagery or neural network graphics can constitute implied health claims

## Absolutely prohibited (RED -- never use)

| Type | Examples |
|------|----------|
| **Medicinal/therapeutic claims** | "Treats brain fog" / "Cures fatigue" / "Prevents cognitive decline" / "Reduces symptoms of ADHD" |
| **Disease risk reduction (unauthorised)** | "Reduces your risk of Alzheimer's" / "Protects against neurodegeneration" |
| **Unauthorised health claims for non-authorised ingredients** | "Ashwagandha reduces stress" / "Alpha GPC supports cognitive function" / "Lion's Mane supports brain health" / "Glutathione protects your cells" |
| **Product-level cognitive claims** | "Boosts brain function" / "Enhances cognition" / "Improves focus" / "Sharpens mental clarity" |
| **Mood/stress claims** | "Reduces stress" / "Calms anxiety" / "Improves mood" (not authorised for any CONKA ingredient) |
| **Health affected by not consuming** | "Don't let cognitive fog hold you back -- take CONKA" / "Without this your brain function will decline" |
| **Individual doctor recommendations** | "Recommended by Dr Smith" / "Recommended by neuroscientists" (unless an entire professional body endorses) |
| **Testimonials describing health outcomes** | Customer quotes like "This cleared my brain fog" -- this IS a health claim and the advertiser is responsible |
| **Comparative efficacy** | "More effective than..." / "Better than coffee at improving focus" |
| **Mechanism-of-action as product claims** | "Boosts cerebral blood flow" / "Generates AMPK for energy" / "Provides glutathione precursors for recovery" |
| **"Detox"** | Not an authorised claim under any regulation |

## Traffic light system for copy decisions

### GREEN -- safe to use

- Exact EFSA authorised claim wordings for Vitamin C and B12 (marked with `††`)
- Minor rewording that conveys the same meaning as an authorised claim
- Factual statements about what ingredients the product contains
- Factual statements about form/source ("Contains KSM-66 ashwagandha extract")
- Dosage information per serving
- Format/convenience statements ("A 30ml liquid shot")
- Study references using observational framing: "In one study, participants showed..."
- General wellbeing claims ONLY when accompanied by a specific authorised claim on the same page
- Mandatory information statements (varied diet, do not exceed dose, etc.)

### AMBER -- requires careful handling

- "Scientifically formulated" -- only if the science behind the formulation is substantiable
- "Clinically studied ingredients" -- only if the specific extracts at specific doses are studied (claim is about existence of studies, not effect)
- "Clinically dosed" -- must add anchor (`†`) and substantiate that doses match published studies
- Lemon balm claims -- "on hold" botanical status makes this a grey area
- "Premium" / "clinical grade" -- vague quality claims that must be defensible
- "Nootropic" as a category descriptor -- borderline; safer to avoid implying cognitive enhancement
- Comparative claims ("more bioavailable than X") -- must be substantiated

### RED -- do not use

Everything listed in the "Absolutely prohibited" section above.

## Mandatory website statements (distance selling)

Every product page must include:

- [ ] Recommended daily dose / portion
- [ ] "Do not exceed the stated recommended daily dose"
- [ ] "Food supplements should not be used as a substitute for a varied and balanced diet and a healthy lifestyle"
- [ ] "Keep out of reach of young children"
- [ ] Nutritional information per daily portion (absolute + %NRV for vitamins/minerals)
- [ ] Full ingredient list
- [ ] Any health claims accompanied by: importance of varied diet, quantity/pattern of consumption for benefit
- [ ] Warnings for specific populations where applicable (pregnant, medication)

## Anchor symbol reference

| Symbol | Category | Meaning |
|--------|----------|---------|
| `†` | Clinically dosed | Dosages match published studies on individual ingredients |
| `††` | EFSA claims | Vitamin C + B12 authorised health claims |
| `‡` | Reviews | Loox verified post-purchase review collection |
| `§` | Bottles sold | Cumulative sales figure as of date |
| `¶` | Research | Ingredient-level studies, not product-level |
| `^^` | Cognitive test | Cognetivity test -- FDA cleared, Cambridge, 93% sensitivity |
| `*` | Guarantee | Satisfaction guarantee terms |

## The three-part test (apply to every piece of copy)

1. **Is there a claim?** Does the text, imagery, or overall impression state, suggest, or imply a relationship between the product (or any ingredient) and health? If yes, go to step 2.

2. **Is the claim authorised?** Is it on the EFSA register for an ingredient in the product at the dose present? If not, it must be removed or reframed. If yes, go to step 3.

3. **Are the mandatory statements present?** Is the claim accompanied by all required information (varied diet statement, portion info, etc.)? If not, add them.

**If a claim fails at any step, it must be rewritten or removed.**
